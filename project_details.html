Modification of Online News Articles to Instagram Posts

Skills: Python (pandas, numpy, seaborn, matplotlib, plotly, nltk, statsmodels), LLMs, Web scraping, Sentiment analysis, Embeddings, Visualization, Communication theory

Instagram is gaining larger importance as a medium of news consumption. However, exactly how news is produced and circulated on Instagram is understudied. I collected a dataset of 19,000 pairs of online news articles and their modifications into Instagram news posts through web scraping and data cleaning to explore how headlines and body texts of news articles are modified into posts.

Through VADER sentiment analysis, text embeddings and edit distance analysis, I found that while headlines did not show a systematic pattern in modification, body texts were shorter and more emotionally neutral in Instagram news posts than in original articles.

Moreover, I adopted an innovative approach to identifying news frames in text by using OpenAI's GPT 3.5-turbo model to read news articles and answer Yes/No questions to detect the presence of news frames . I found that frames that determine the emotional tone of a news story are frequently kept while an article is shortened into a post.

This project was completed as an undergraduate thesis project in New York University Abu Dhabi, Social Research and Public Policy program with Professor Minsu Park. I received the Social Science Capstone Award of Excellence for oustanding research.

School Shooting and Academic Performance

Skills: Python (pandas, numpy, seaborn, matplotlib, stats), Matching, Regression, Descriptive statistics

School shooting is one of the most discussed topics in the United States, especially as it may directly affect young children. In this project, I merged Washington Post's dataset on 387 school shootings with annual math and reading test scores dataset from the U.S. Department of Education to investigate the influence of a shooting incident on student academic performance.

Using coarsened exact matching, we found that schools with a shooting incident tend to have a lower academic performance after the incident, compared to other schools without an incident in the same time period. Through a matching experiment with t-tests , we found that shootings tend to decrease the educational quality of the school itself, rather than only affecting the students at the time. Our final multi-linear regression analysis demonstrated that racially diverse schools were more heavily impacted by shootings, compared to white-majority schools.

This project was completed as a final group project in the "Computational Social Science" course in New York University Abu Dhabi with Professor Talal Rahwan.

Car Crashes and Driver Sex

Skills: Python (pandas, numpy, seaborn, matplotlib, stats), Supervised machine learning (Linear SVM, Decision Tree, Logistic Regression, Random Forest), Data visualizations

To provide concrete answers to conflicting evidence on the relationship between driver gender and dangerous driving behaviors, we used data on all car crashes in Chicago to test the hypothesis that a driver's gender has an influence on the possibility of an injury in case of a car accident.

First, feature selection was performed to choose variables to include in our machine learning models. Our first attempt on building regression models (linear regression with Ridge and Lasso regularization, Linear SVM, and Decision Tree regression) were not suitable to represent the data. Hence, we used classification models (Linear SVM, Logistic regression, Decision tree, and Random forest) to find that female drivers are less likely to cause injuries in a car crash.

This project was completed as a final group project in the "Applied Data Science" course in New York University Abu Dhabi with Professor Bedoor AlShebli.

Best Selling Books and New York Times Articles

Skills: R, Python, Topic modeling, Sentiment analysis, Wordnet

Statistics show that people read non-fiction books to keep up with current events. I further investigate into this by exploring the relationship between best-sellers and popular New York Times articles.

I used the New York Times API and dataset on New York Times bestsellers to identify non-fiction best-sellers since 1931. Then I found the headline and lead paragraphs of New York Times articles to use as a proxy for current events at each time period.

Using Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modeling on book titles and article headlines, I found that while war and crime are common topics in both, best-selling books tend to be about personal, emotional topics, while articles tend to be about business-related issues.

I also conducted VADER sentiment analysis and abstractness analysis using Wordnet and found that the writing style in books and articles were similar until the 1980s, after which books became more negative and concrete, compared to articles. This suggests that keeping up with the world is not the only reason for reading books, as many people read about topics outside of public discourse.

This project was completed as a final project in the "Textual Analysis for Social Sciences" course in New York University Abu Dhabi with Professor Minsu Park.

Modeling Educational Attainment with Economic Indicators

Skills: Stata, Multi-linear regression, Hypothesis testing, Panel data analysis, Macroeconomic theory

Ensuring high educational attainment of students is an important issue for policy makers around the world. Here, we examined the relationship between education and various socio-economic factors.

I used the PISA score dataset from OECD as a proxy for educational attainment. Independent variables included world happiness index and various macroeconomic indices such as GDP per capita, GINI inequality, and money spent on education.

I first did a multi-linear regression analysis using data from 2018 among 38 countries. However, GDP per capita, number of teaching hours, and inequality were all statistically insignificant in predicting educational attainment, although they were jointly significant.

To investigate deeper, the dataset was expanded into a panel dataset of 2015 and 2018 data from the same 38 countries. Here, we found that none of the indicators were statistically significant, implying a need for deeper investigation into the data. Through close examination of the educational attainment and relevant policies in specific countries, we hypothesized that equity in education is an important variable in predicting educational attainment. We found that an increase in equity was indeed statistically significantly relate to an increase in educational attainment, emphasizing the importance of policies to ensure equity in education.

This project was completed as a final group project in the "Data Analysis for Economics" course in New York University Abu Dhabi with Professor Betul Arda Bas.
